What's Behind The Growing Air Quality Complaints in Queens

More New Yorkers are calling in concerns about indoor chemical odors in the World's borough

By Maja Clasen

Monday | November 27, 2023

New Yorkers are no strangers to poor air quality. Wildfires in Canada blew harmful smoke into the City just earlier this summer, turning the sky into a hazy blur. But it’s not just the great outdoors that can be hazardous. Since 2020, every borough except Staten Island has seen an increase in indoor air quality complaints to 311.

These complaints include a range of concerns, from building renovation dust to chemical odors.



While Queens only had the third highest overall number complaints in 2021, it experienced the largest single-year increase from 2020-2021. City records show a whopping 114% increase in complaints. But that dramatic surge wasn’t coming from all residents equally. A majority of the growing complaints came from people living in the neighborhoods of Ridgewood, Maspath, Middle Village, and Glendale — Which together make up Queens Community Board District 5. That trend continued into 2022 as well.



Reports of chemical fumes and poor ventilation were most common across the last three full-years of data. These concerns should be taken seriously, because they can point to potential health risks. And about 168,000 people live in this area, according to the Furman Center.

According to the NYC Department of Health, “Indoor air pollution comes from smoking, cooking, household cleaning products, poor ventilation and other building maintenance problems. While poor air quality affects us all, older adults, children and people with heart and lung conditions are most at risk.”



It’s difficult to pinpoint why there are so many complaints all of a sudden. But there are some possible hints.

Previous monitoring has shown that air pollution is slightly worse in this district than the borough and City as a whole. That could be due, in part, to the fact that Ridgewood and Middle Village are high and medium level industrial areas, releasing more noxious fumes into the environment.

Another possible contributor is poor housing maintenance. Serious housing code violations in the district peaked in 2021 with 54.3 violations per 1,000 rental units, before coming down slightly in 2022.

If nothing changes, Queens – led by District 5 – could surpass Brooklyn to have the most indoors air quality reports.
